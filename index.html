About me

Ecological economist working on how food systems and agricultural trade generate, displace, and obscure costs to land and soil ecosystems.

I am a PhD candidate in economics at Northwest A&F University. My research is in ecological economics, where the central question is not only how land is used but what happens to the living systems within it. Soil biological communities, nutrient cycles, and landscape-level ecosystem functioning underpin global agricultural productivity yet remain outside every existing trade and environmental accounting framework. I study how agricultural production generates costs to these systems, how those costs are embodied in trade flows and displaced across borders, and how consumption-based accounting can be extended to make the belowground dimension of global food systems visible and governable.

My core interests are land ecological security, belowground ecosystem multifunctionality, consumption-based environmental accounting, and the equity dimensions of ecological burden displacement through trade. The thread connecting them is a conviction that the functional biology of landscapes is both foundational to food systems and systematically undervalued in the economic frameworks that govern how land and trade are managed. If your work touches on any of these themes, I welcome the conversation through the contact form.
